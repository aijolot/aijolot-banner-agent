A mid-page section banner can act as a positive visual stop or as a wall that ends the session — heatmaps tell you which. Read three maps together: a scroll map shows the average fold and the % of users reaching 25/50/75% depth, exposing a "false bottom" where a banner's large whitespace or hard divider makes users think the page has ended and they stop scrolling; a click/tap map shows whether the banner's CTA actually gets engaged; and a move/rage-click map flags friction — dense cursor movement with no clicks (confusion about what's interactive) or repeated rage-clicks. If a scroll map shows users dropping off before reaching key content below a banner, either move that content up or strengthen the reason to continue past the banner. Judge a section banner by whether traffic flows *through* it (continued scroll + CTA clicks), not merely by impressions — a banner everyone scrolls past, or stops at, is failing regardless of how it looks.
