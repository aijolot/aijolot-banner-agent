A converting hero is built copy-first as a "typeframe" — a low-fidelity wireframe with high-fidelity text — so the message, hierarchy, and spacing are nailed before any pixels of imagery exist; designing words to fit a chosen background image is the backwards process that produces beautiful-but-empty heroes. The proven element stack is: a kicker (social proof, e.g. client numbers), a brief crystal-clear headline that names the visitor's problem/solution, a description that pre-empts 1–2 common objections, and one primary CTA (add a secondary CTA only for high-ticket/high-friction offers). For imagery, ~99% of businesses convert better with a real human than with abstract or AI-generated backgrounds, because people let the viewer imagine themselves in the situation — and the human's gaze should point toward the CTA, not at the camera. Reinforce with guiding lines (break text to create diagonal flow toward the button) and subtly color-matching one element of the photo to the CTA color. Heroes fail when aesthetics are decoupled from the message: mysterious copy, generic stock photos, and multiple competing CTAs.
