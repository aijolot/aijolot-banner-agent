title: "Use directional visual cues — arrows, gaze direction, encapsulation — to steer the eye to the section CTA"

  applicable_when: "mid-page section banners and any banner whose goal is a single click"
---

Visual cues literally point the visitor at the element you want them to act on. CXL's eye-tracking study built identical landing pages differing only in cue: an explicit hand-drawn arrow pointing at the form produced the longest time on page and concentrated attention on the form, while a photo of a person looking *away* from the form drew the least attention and interaction. Two cue families work together — explicit cues (arrows, lines) spread attention across the whole pointed-at region and are strongest for grabbing it, while a person's eye-gaze cue directs attention precisely to the spot being looked at. So in a section banner with a human subject, orient their gaze toward the headline or CTA, never out of frame. Reinforce with implicit cues: encapsulation (a frame/container around the CTA), color contrast, and whitespace that isolates the button. The takeaway for the banner agent: every section banner should contain at least one cue aimed at its single CTA, and any person depicted must look toward — not away from — the action.
